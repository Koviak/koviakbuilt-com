# Designing for the Texas Climate: What You Need to Know

Texas weather is not subtle. Summers bring sustained triple-digit heat, spring delivers severe thunderstorms and hail, and winter can surprise you with ice storms that shut down the grid. A luxury custom home in the Hill Country needs to be designed from the ground up to handle all of it gracefully.

**Orientation and overhangs** are your first line of defense against the Texas sun. The west side of your home takes the most punishing afternoon heat, so minimizing glass on that elevation -- or protecting it with deep overhangs, covered porches, or motorized shade screens -- dramatically reduces cooling loads. South-facing glass is actually easier to manage because the high summer sun angle allows relatively short overhangs to block direct light while still admitting winter sun for passive warmth.

**Roofing material matters more than most people realize.** A standing-seam metal roof in a light color reflects significantly more solar radiation than traditional composition shingles. Metal roofs also perform better in hail, have a longer lifespan, and shed water more effectively during heavy downpours. We recommend 26-gauge standing seam as a minimum for our custom homes, and many clients choose to upgrade to a heavier gauge for additional durability.

**Moisture management** is critical even in the relatively dry Hill Country. Seasonal humidity swings, driving rain, and the temperature differentials between air-conditioned interiors and hot exteriors create conditions that can lead to moisture intrusion if the building envelope is not properly detailed. We use rain screens behind stone and siding, carefully integrated housewrap systems, and spray foam insulation that doubles as an air barrier to keep moisture where it belongs -- outside.

**Backup power** has become a standard feature in our builds since Winter Storm Uri demonstrated the vulnerability of the Texas grid. Whole-house generators with automatic transfer switches ensure that your HVAC, refrigeration, well pump, and security systems continue operating regardless of what is happening on the grid.
